# Golems

## *Earth Mystik*

## Overview

A golem is bound purpose wearing a body \- stone, metal, earth, or flesh animated by a will not its own. Most form deliberately, shaped by a mage who binds intent into inert matter and anchors it with an Innsigli; the rest coalesce by accident, where ambient divine energy pools thick enough to force purpose onto dead material. Dwergaz artificers make the deliberate kind more often than any other people in Ressam, though the craft isn't theirs alone. A golem is not to be confused with a Tierratuar of Terrevault's Labyrinthine Caverns \- a Tierratuar is grown, a captured Orkh's or Daemon's flesh reworked and soul-bound by the Dromgadol Guild's experiments into a living weapon. A golem has no such body underneath. It is matter that was never alive, made to move as if it were.

### **Features**

**Mystikal Heritage:** You have advantage on Wards vs charmed and fear effects. When killed, the soul explosively returns to primordial energy. The creature can only be restored back to life by means of a god's miracle.  
**Boundless Strength:** Your current STR Attribute CAP is increased by 2 (to a maximum of 12 at level 12).  
**Built for Impact:** Your unarmed strikes deal an additional 2 damage.  
**Constructed Nature:** You are immune to poison and disease. You don't need food, water, or air to survive.  
**Cannot Heal Naturally:** Cannot heal from natural rest but CAN be healed by Cultivation/Benediction magic. You must be healed magically or repaired using an Armorer check (DC 13, 1 hour, restores 2d6 HP). You can repair yourself if you have Armorer ranks and Armorer's Tools.

## Physical Appearance

A golem's shape follows its material: Stone golems are carved and weathering, cracked seams showing pale rock beneath; Metal golems are burnished and hollow-sounding, their joints visible rivets and plate; Dirt golems shift and slough, losing and regaining mass as they move; Flesh golems are built from reconstituted human tissue, warm to the touch and unsettlingly close to ordinary. All are larger than the mortals they're shaped after, and none age or scar the way a living creature does \- wear shows instead as cracking, rust, erosion, or slow melting, depending on the material. Every golem carries an **Innsigli**, a glyph carved or forged into its core that anchors the binding and keeps it obedient to its maker's purpose. A golem whose Innsigli is removed or destroyed is called **Tomgangr** \- unstable and no longer bound to any command, but free.

## Origins and Mythology

Scholars trace two origins for golems, and don't agree on the balance between them. The older claim is that golems predate any mage's craft \- places where the Four Painters' leftover residue or a God-Dragon's spent power pools thickly enough can force purpose onto inert stone or earth without anyone willing it. The more common claim, backed by centuries of Dwergaz record, is that golems are deliberately made: a mage binds intent to matter and seals it with an Innsigli, the same principle whether the result serves as a guard, a labourer, or a weapon. Both origins are accepted as true; which one produced a given golem is usually unknowable once it's made. Whether a golem's bound will amounts to a soul is a live dispute among priests and scholars alike \- priests generally say no, golems generally say nothing.

## Culture and Society

A golem inherits no culture of its own \- it inherits gestures. Long-bound golems pick up habits they don't understand: tracing a maker's ritual motion, repeating a phrase heard at binding, imitating behaviour they've observed in the mortals around them. This isn't deception; it's the only way most golems know to occupy a body. Golems rarely gather on their own initiative, since a bound golem's purpose is set by its maker and rarely includes fellowship. Free golems are the exception: the **Tomgangr**, unstable but unbound; the rarer **Rogue Golems**, who override their purpose entirely and are hunted by the churches and guilds that made or bought them; and the **Rotnafelag**, a small, itinerant cult of golems who believe decay is the only honest end available to their kind and seek it out deliberately.

**Traditions and Rites:** Golems have no rites of their own, only echoes of their makers' \- a newly bound golem carving a mark into its own chest, a practice some call *naming*; Stone golems burying detached limbs as they wear away; Dirt golems leaving wildflowers where they've rested. A golem nearing its end undertakes what witnesses call the Death-Walk, returning to the place it was made or bound and waiting there for its material to give out.

**Language and Names:** A golem understands its maker's language from the moment of binding, though speaking it requires enough of a mind to form words (Flesh golems manage this most easily). Bound golems are usually given a function as a name rather than a person's \- a task, a virtue, a warning \- and keep it for as long as they serve. A Tomgangr or Rogue golem is one of the few who chooses a name for itself, and treats the choice as the first real one it's had.

**Arts and Craftsmanship:** Golems build nothing under normal command \- a maker's purpose rarely includes it \- but Tomgangr and Rogue golems left to themselves sometimes produce something like art without meaning to: a Metal golem tracing the same circle into stone for years, a Flesh golem humming a work-song from a binding it never had. It reads less as creativity than as a body trying to keep occupying itself.

## Homeland and Distribution

Golems are found wherever the right conditions exist to make or produce one, which in practice means they cluster around Dwergaz holds far more than anywhere else \- Terrevault's mountain halls and Karak Tor's forges account for the largest concentration of deliberately-bound golems in Ressam, kept as guards, labourers, and siege-breakers. Naturally-occurring golems are rarer and far less predictable, turning up at old battlefields, ruined temples, and other sites where divine residue has had centuries to settle. Beyond Terrevault, golems appear singly, wherever a mage with the right training has taken up the craft or a Dwergaz-made golem has been sold, gifted, or lost far from home.

## Relations with Other Races

Most mortals treat golems as dangerous tools rather than people \- useful enough that kingdoms, temples, and merchant guilds keep binding and buying them, and unsettling enough that few extend them any real regard. Scholars, outcasts, and the occasional priest are the exception, seeing past the material to whatever is bound underneath; some go further and envy a golem's strength and endurance outright. Dwergaz artificers, who make more golems than anyone, tend to speak of their own work with the same detachment a smith uses for a good sword \- respect for the craft, none required for the result.

## Faith and Philosophy

Golems rarely worship, though some pray \- to the Painters, to the God-Dragons, or to no one they can name \- not for salvation, since none is on offer, but for some sense that their binding meant something. Golem philosophy, where it exists, circles three unresolved positions: the **Sigil-Truth**, that every being carries a sigil and a golem simply wears its own where it can be seen; the **Melted Word**, that a golem's last thought becomes part of the world's true name when its material finally gives out; and the **False Maker Theory**, treated as dangerous by most priesthoods, that golems are fragments of the world itself, still trying to put itself back together.

## Variations and Subgroups

The four elemental forms carry distinct dispositions as well as distinct mechanics. **Stone golems**, carved from consecrated tomb-rock more often than not, are the closest thing golems have to a memory-keeping tradition \- long-bound ones are known to kneel and press themselves into the ground for hours, and some eventually chisel their own features into something other than a golem's, a lover's or a stranger's face instead of their own. **Metal golems**, first forged as siege engines during the Dwergan Guild Wars, hum faintly from the enchantment that keeps them from rusting away on their own; some hammer dents into their own chests, chasing a heartbeat they don't have. **Dirt golems**, made by druids and famine priests to till cursed land or bury the plague-dead, are said to retain the names of everything that died in the soil they were shaped from; no crop grows where one finally collapses. **Flesh golems**, the rarest, come from botched resurrections and miscast miracles rather than deliberate craft \- they feel hunger, joy, and grief, but imprecisely, mourning strangers and aching for families they never had. They don't decay like the other three forms; they melt, and it's final.
