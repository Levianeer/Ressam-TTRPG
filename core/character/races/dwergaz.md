# Dwergaz

## *Earthborne Artefolk*

## Overview

The Dwergaz are a race of stoic, earth-bound beings - cut, not born. Every Dwergaz was hewn by his clan's own rites from a living vein of stone, a son of the Great Mother and of no one else. The veins yield slowly, and fewer with each passing century, and everything the Dwergaz are follows from that arithmetic: a people who cannot replace their dead build machines to fall in their place, walls that outlast sieges, and wealth enough to make war against them unprofitable. Masters of craft and keepers of grudges, they value endurance over ephemeral glory, building not just fortresses but legacies. Though their gruff exteriors and xenophobic tendencies alienate outsiders, their subterranean cities thrive where trade, artistry, and vengeance intertwine - all beneath the rule of the Tor'Az Triumvirate, a guild-throne of unmatched industrial and military might.

**Grudge Bearer:** Once per Field Rest or Long Rest, when a creature deals damage to you or an ally, harms your property, or breaks a formal agreement with you, you may declare a grudge against them (no action required). You gain \+2 to attack and damage rolls against your grudge target. The grudge ends when the target dies, or when you declare a new grudge. You can have only one grudge at a time.

## Physical Appearance

The Dwergaz are a broad and compact people, and an exclusively male one \- the Mother has never cut a daughter free, and no Dwergaz thinks to ask why, any more than he would ask why stone is hard. They average 4-4 1/2 feet tall, with dense muscle and bone akin to bedrock, earth-toned skin ranging from granite grey to clay red, and eyes like polished gemstones \- commonly amber or emerald. Faint mineral veining runs beneath every Dwergaz's skin, the grain of the seam that yielded him; vein-kin can recognize one another by it at a glance, and reading a stranger's grain is considered as intimate as reading his letters.

A Dwergaz emerges from the vein rough \- his features blunt, his voice unset, his grain still bright. Over his first four decades the stone of him *cures*: features sharpen like weathered carving, and adulthood at 40 is marked by a ritual first craft or oath, the moment a Dwergaz stops being shaped and begins to shape. Slow to age, they live 300-400 years. Their thick hair is often braided with metal rings or rune-carved beads, and their hands are calloused from labour.

## Origins and Mythology

The Dwergaz are sons of the Great Mother \- the living moon whose slow passes knead the deep stone of Ressam, and whose essence pools in the veins from which all Dwergaz are cut. On this every clan agrees. On *which* moon is the Mother, no two clans have ever agreed, and grudges have been carved over less.

Orthodox myth holds that in the deep of the first age, the Mother's touch quickened the stone at the roots of Karak's Spine, and Melacha \- her eldest son and her hand \- took up the first chisel and cut his brothers free from the seam. Their souls were imbued with the echoes of Nekama's first oath: "To overcome is to remember." Myths tell of these first-cut sons tunnelling to the heart of Ressam to meet Mischar, who gifted them the first scales \- balanced for trade, tipped for war.

The veins the Mother quickened, the Mat'dûn, still live \- but they are finite, they cannot be made, and they are slowly running dry. Whether this is a trial of endurance, a punishment, or simply the way of all things is the great unanswered question of Dwergaz theology. Heretical sects claim the race was instead forged in the fires of a betrayed god's wrath, and that the failing of the veins is the slow completion of a curse \- which would, they note, explain the grudges.

## Culture and Society

**Social Structure:** Guilds rule all, with the Tor'Az Triumvirate as the pinnacle. There are no bloodlines \- there is no blood to line. Kinship is of the vein: a Dwergaz's clan is the seam he was cut from, his brothers those quarried from the same grain, his elders those cut in earlier yields. Status is earned through craft or combat alone.

**The Cutting:** New Dwergaz are hewn from the Mat'dûn by rite, on nights determined by each clan's lunar creed. The veins cannot be rushed, coaxed, or expanded; a rich seam may yield a handful of sons in a decade, a failing one none in a generation. The location of a birthing-vein is the most closely guarded secret in any hold \- to reveal one to an outsider is the sole crime in Khazduul with no word for its punishment, because no punishment has ever been considered sufficient to name.

**Stonebinding:** The oath-bond of two Dwergaz \- shield-brothers, craft-partners, lovers; the rite does not distinguish and no Dwergaz would think the distinction meaningful. The pair carve interlocking rings from a single geode, two forms cut from one stone, as they themselves were.

**The Returning:** The dead are crushed into gemstone dust. The honoured are carried down and given back to the Mat'dûn itself, in the hope that the Mother reuses what she is given \- whether she does is a question the priests refuse to answer. The rest are set into the mortar of the clan hall, so that the hold is, quite literally, built of its ancestors.

**Grudge Annals:** Every slight is recorded in lead-bound tomes by the Bookkeepers of Terrevault. Outsiders mistake this for spite. It is accounting. The Mother will not cut the same son twice; a Dwergaz death is therefore not a loss but a debt that can never be repaid in kind \- and the Annals are the ledger of a people who do not forgive debts, least of all ones whose principal was irreplaceable.

**Language and Names:** *Khazduul*, a guttural tongue with no word for "forgive." Names reflect deeds (e.g., Ironhand) or crafts (e.g., Dwarin means "unbroken anvil"). Names carry no gender \- a race of only sons needs none.

**Arts and Craftsmanship:** Renowned for living engines \- bridges that self-repair, siege engines powered by harvested daemon souls. Dwergaz doctrine holds that machines exist to stand where Dwergaz would otherwise fall; every automaton fielded is a son who did not have to be spent. Their engineers speak of "the arithmetic": one vein-cut life is worth any quantity of iron.

## Homeland and Distribution

The Dwergaz's primary hold is the subterranean empire of Tor'Az, anchored by Karak's Spine (capital) and Tradehaven (a decadent, cosmopolitan port city carved into a cavern ceiling).

Every great hold is built around a moonwell \- a vertical shaft, bored kilometres deep with obsessive precision, aligned so that on the correct nights the light of the correct moon falls unbroken to touch the central temple below. The holiest object in every Dwergaz hold is a hole. A hold whose moonwell is collapsed, captured, or knocked out of true by seismic shift is a hold in mortal crisis, whatever its walls still stand. Tradehaven, hanging from its cavern ceiling, has no well at all \- one more reason the deep clans regard it as gloriously profane.

Beyond the great holds, remote mining outposts dot Ressam's mountain ranges, often clashing with surface-dwellers over mineral rights.

## Relations with Other Races

Allies are few. Tradehaven's neutrality forces temporary partnerships with merchant leagues, and the Dwergaz arm nearly everyone \- selling siege engines even to enemies of their customers \- on the doctrine that a people who cannot grow their numbers must grow their leverage, and that arming your enemies' enemies is cheaper than bleeding. Their enemies are clearer: the Orkhaden Horde, accused of stealing Mischar's first scales, and the Aelves, seen as flighty "rootless twigs."

The strangest of their bonds is also their closest: the Feliids of the Tagarian Coalition. On its face the pairing is absurd - the most guarded people in Ressam and the most flamboyant - but the Dwergaz long ago noticed that a maritime race is the one great power that cannot covet what lies beneath the stone. The Feliids meet them at Tradehaven and Sokhara, never ask to go deeper, and honor their contracts to the letter; in return, Tagarian fleets carry Dwergaz engines to every coast, and Tagarian blades bleed where Dwergaz doctrine forbids Dwergaz to. And there is a quieter trade beneath the commerce: Feliid captains pay the Bookkeepers of Terrevault to enter their names and deeds into the lead-bound tomes - not grudges, legacies - and the Dwergaz, who cannot forget, keep them. In the vaults beneath Karak's Spine lie the ancestral memories of entire Feliid families, tended by stone men who will still be reading those names two centuries after the last grandchild forgot them.

"Stubborn as a Dwergaz grudge" is an accurate stereotype; "greedy as a Mischar pirate" is less so \- their wealth is reinvested, not hoarded. Outsiders also believe the Dwergaz hide their women underground. The Dwergaz find this rumour useful and have never once corrected it.

## Faith and Philosophy

Dwergaz faith is as enduring as stone \- quiet, resolute, and rooted in action over prayer. Above and beneath all stands the **Great Mother**, who is one of the twin moons. She is not prayed to, any more than one prays to the ground one stands on; she is *attended*. Her passes are tracked, her tremors read \- to the Dwergaz, Ressam's frequent quakes are not geology but her touch, the Mother kneading the deep stone, and every Dwergaz seismologist is a priest reading scripture in real time.

The surface races call the moons Patéras and Mitéra \- the Father who leads and the Mother who trails. The Dwergaz do not accept surface astronomy as authoritative about their own Mother, and have split into three great creeds. **The Trailing Creed**, orthodox in Terrevault, holds that the trailing moon is the Mother, as even the surface-folk dimly perceived: she follows because she gathers, the hand that comes after the strike, and the veins yield to her patience. **The Leading Creed** holds that the surface races named the moons exactly backwards \- an error the Dwergaz find entirely unsurprising \- and that the leading moon is the Mother: she goes first as the chisel goes before the hand, and calling her "Father" is a surface superstition that mistakes precedence for masculinity. **The Creed of the Embrace** holds that the Mother is neither light alone but the conjunction \- the alignment when the moons' combined pull runs deepest and the tremors are strongest; clans of this creed cut new sons free only during the Embrace, and their yields are the rarest and, they insist, the truest.

In Khazduul, the Mother is *Matka* \- but which physical moon the word points to depends entirely on the speaker's creed, while the other light is dismissed as *the Herald*. Two Dwergaz can swear by Matka and mean different moons. Outsiders find this maddening. The Dwergaz consider it a feature. Because the creeds disagree on which passes quicken the veins, they disagree on when the Cutting may be performed \- a doctrinal dispute that has started more than one guild war.

Beneath the Mother stand her three eldest, the first-cut sons and daughter she raised up \- the **Dwergan Triad**: Mischar, Nekama, and Melacha. **For their full domains, see the Religions chapter.** Mischar is the Mother's one daughter, raised from the heart of the world; Nekama urges Dwergaz never to forget, his Grudge Annals binding justice to memory; Melacha, the Mother's hand, cut the first Dwergaz free and teaches that true devotion lies in the pursuit of mastery.

A host of lesser deities crowd beneath them, from the God of Cupboards to the God of Mines. Worship is woven into daily life \- engraved into tools, oaths, and guild rituals. Temples are austere, part shrine, part vault, where offerings are made in craft and deed.

Above all, Dwergaz spirituality prizes endurance: of body, memory, and legacy. To forget is to fail. To strive is to honor the gods. And to create, avenge, and trade well is the truest prayer. But beneath the endurance lies the question no creed has answered: the veins are failing, and the Mother has not said why. The Trailing Creed calls it a trial. The heretics call it a curse. And in Tormaz, some have stopped asking the Mother anything at all.

## Variations and Subgroups

Though cut from the same Mother, the Dwergaz have split into two branches: the Durmhezzar of Terrevault and the Dromvalkaz of Tormaz. The Durmhezzar cling to tradition, living in the deep halls of the Tor'Az Triumvirate where craft is sacred, grudges are law, and every chisel mark must echo perfection. Their aesthetic is stoic and geometric \- living architecture, rune-forged tools, and ancestral legacies sealed in stone. They treat souls with reverence, binding them only into shrines or family relics, and they hold that the failing of the veins is the Mother's trial: to endure it is worship, and to circumvent it is unthinkable.

In contrast, the Dromvalkaz of Tormaz live in the shadow of the Infernal Gate, shaped by unrelenting war and strange energies. Their skin bears ashen tones and ember-veined scars, their machines hiss with soul-bound fire, and their creations \- like the Tierratuar \- blur the line between life and artifice. Led by Dwarin the Industrious and the experimental Dromgadol Guild, they prize invention above sanctity, and they answer Terrevault's trial-theology with a colder creed: a race that waits to die deserves to. Where Terrevault sees heresy, Tormaz sees evolution \- each strike of the hammer not remembering the past, but forging the future.
