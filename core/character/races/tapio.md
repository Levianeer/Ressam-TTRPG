# Tapio

## *Water Mystik*

## Overview

The Tapio are sorrow-bound caretakers of forgotten wilds \- mystikal beings born not of flesh, but of memory and grief. Formed from the First Forest, the final dream of the Four Great Artists, the Tapio remember a world that no longer is. They are guardians of beauty long lost, embodiments of mourning so deep it takes root in the land itself. Where neglect festers and nature falls silent, a Tapio may emerge \- not to reclaim what was lost, but to bear witness. To encounter one is to be seen by the forest itself, and to feel, however briefly, the weight of a vanished age pressing against the skin.

## Physical Appearance

Tapio are eerie and plantlike, twisted echoes of humanoid life shaped by vine, bark, and sorrow. Skin resembles living wood, rough as bark or smooth as driftwood; blood runs thick golden sap, tendons knotted vine. Limbs are long and branch-clawed, tails of ivy or moss trail behind, and faces are feral and half-formed. No two are alike, shaped by the place and pain that birthed them, and their bodies wither only when their spirit fades, never by age.

## Origins and Mythology

Born of the First Forest, the final fragile dream of the Four Artists, the Tapio were never meant to shape the world, only to care for it \- relics of divinity born from the resonance of sacred loss rather than seed or root. When the Artists fell into slumber and the world broke, the Tapio remained as living echoes of that original beauty. Each Tapio soul is a seed of the First Forest, and their greatest belief is that sorrow is sacred \- not weakness, but proof that something once mattered.

## Culture and Society

Tapio form no nations, clans, or cities, moving like wind or water toward places heavy with memory \- overgrown ruins, abandoned gardens, lands of forgotten tragedy. They are solitary rather than hermits, drawn together rarely and only with solemn purpose.

**Rites and Traditions:** Tapio do not marry or reproduce \- they emerge spontaneously in forsaken places, their births marked by sudden flourishing (flowers out of season, overnight moss). Their deaths are natural phenomena too: the earth responds, plants sing briefly with joy, and the forest consumes them entirely, their name lost but their presence not.

**Language and Names:** Tapio speak a lilting, musical tongue known only to them and the wind, communicating to other races by projecting directly into the mind. Names aren't given at birth \- they are found, whispered by trees or shaped by experience.

**Art and Expression:** Tapio paint with pollen and dye with leaf-oil in images that fade with the seasons, sing melancholic songs to animals and rivers, carve moonlit runes into trees, and build sculptures from root and memory.

## Homeland and Distribution

Tapio emerge in isolated places \- deep woods, crumbled temples, fog-choked glades \- most often in the hollows of the Greater Groves and the plains of Halig. They sometimes tend a "weeping ground," a place of deep spiritual ache, for decades, but are bound to the land: when the places they haunt are healed or destroyed, they vanish. Some wander briefly into cities, drawn by a melody or shared pain, but rarely stay.

## Relations with Other Races

To most, Tapio are unsettling at best, terrifying at worst \- children's tales paint them as spirits who steal or lure away the lost, though other stories tell of Tapio saving travellers or offering cryptic wisdom to artists and poets. The Aelves respect them deeply, especially the Ash'shene, who see them as proof that even divine beauty must decay; Dwergaz find them uncomfortably abstract, though some in the Triumvirate preserve their funerary songs as sacred curiosities. Tapio rarely initiate contact but are rarely hostile \- to them, most mortals are bright little fires: brief, tragic, beautiful.

## Faith and Philosophy

Tapio do not worship, they remember \- the closest thing they have to faith is reverence for what was, a passive devotion to the Four Painters not as gods to praise, but as dreamers who made something too beautiful to survive. They believe sorrow is holy and remembrance an act of love, and do not fear death, since death is only the forest recalling what once mattered.
