# Tapio

## *Water Mystik*

## Overview

The Tapio are sorrow-bound caretakers of forgotten wilds \- mystikal beings born not of flesh, but of memory and grief. Formed from the First Forest, the final dream of the Four Great Artists, the Tapio remember a world that no longer is. They are guardians of beauty long lost, embodiments of mourning so deep it takes root in the land itself. Where neglect festers and nature falls silent, a Tapio may emerge \- not to reclaim what was lost, but to bear witness. To encounter one is to be seen by the forest itself, and to feel, however briefly, the weight of a vanished age pressing against the skin.

## Physical Appearance

Tapio are eerie and plantlike in their forms \- twisted echoes of humanoid life shaped by vine, bark, and sorrow. Their skin resembles living wood, rough and patterned like tree bark or smoothed like driftwood, often dark or mossy in hue. Blood is thick golden sap; tendons, knotted vine. Their limbs are long and expressive, ending in branch-like claws or root-knuckled fingers. Tails of wrapped ivy or trailing moss sway behind them. Faces are feral, sometimes half-formed or overly expressive \- animalistic in their emotive power. No two Tapio are exactly alike, shaped as they are by the place and pain that birthed them. They grow slowly and do not age in the mortal sense. Instead, their bodies wither only when their spirit fades.

## Origins and Mythology

Born of the First Forest \- the final, fragile dream of the Four Artists \- the Tapio were never meant to shape the world, only to care for it. They are not mortals but relics of divinity, created not through design but by the resonance of sacred loss. The First Forest was not planted with seed or root, but painted with feeling \- a garden of intention, unspoiled by time. When the Artists fell into slumber and the world broke, the Tapio remained as living echoes of that original, impossible beauty.

Each Tapio soul is a seed of the First Forest. Their myths are not told but lived \- flashes of memory that surface in their actions, dreams, and art. They are drawn to forgotten places and hear whispers in the trees. Their greatest belief is that sorrow is sacred. Not weakness, but proof that something once mattered.

## Culture and Society

Tapio society is not structured in any traditional sense. They do not form nations, clans, or cities. Instead, they move like wind or water, gravitating toward places heavy with memory: overgrown ruins, abandoned gardens, lands steeped in forgotten tragedy. They are not hermits, but they are solitary \- drawn together rarely, and always with solemn purpose.

**Rites and Traditions:** The Tapio do not marry or reproduce. They emerged spontaneously in places so forsaken that only the land itself remembers them. Their births are quiet events, marked by sudden flourishing \- flowers blooming out of season, moss forming overnight, a soft wind that knows your name. Their funerals are natural phenomena: when a Tapio dies, the earth responds, plants sing, the world bends into a brief illusion of joy, and then the forest consumes them entirely. Their name is lost, but not their presence.

**Language and Names:** Tapio speak in a lilting, musical tongue known only to them and the wind. Names are not given at birth \- they are found, often whispered by trees or shaped by experiences. To other races though, they speak into your mind.

**Art and Expression:** The Tapio excel in haunting, ephemeral arts. They paint with pollen and dye with leaf-oil, crafting images that fade with the seasons. Their songs are melancholic, often sung to animals, rivers, or the dead. Music and dreams are sacred languages. Some Tapio carve runes into trees that only show under moonlight, or build sculptures from root and memory.

## Homeland and Distribution

Tapio are never common, and never expected. They emerge in isolated places: deep woods, crumbled temples, fog-choked glades. They are found most often in the hollows of the Greater Groves and the plains of Halig. They do not settle, but sometimes tend to a "weeping ground" \- a place of deep spiritual ache where they linger for decades.

They are inherently bound to the land. When the places they haunt are healed or destroyed, they vanish. Some wander briefly into cities, drawn by a melody or a shared pain, but they rarely stay long.

## Relations with Other Races

To most of the world, the Tapio are unsettling at best, terrifying at worst. Children's stories often paint them as forest spirits who steal kids or lure the lost away. Yet there are tales of Tapio saving travellers, guiding lost souls, or offering cryptic wisdom to artists and poets.

The Aelves respect them deeply, especially the heretical Ash'shene sects who believe the Tapio are proof that even divine beauty must decay. Dwergaz find them uncomfortably abstract, but some among the Triumvirate preserve their funerary songs as sacred curiosities.

Tapio rarely initiate contact, but they are rarely hostile. They simply live on a slower, sadder axis of time. To them, most mortals are bright little fires \- brief, tragic, beautiful.

## Faith and Philosophy

Tapio do not worship in the conventional sense. They remember. The closest thing they have to faith is reverence for what was, especially that which has been forgotten. In this way, they reflect a kind of passive devotion to the Four Painters \- not as gods to be praised, but as dreamers who created something too beautiful to survive.

They believe sorrow is holy, and remembrance is an act of love. They do not fear death, for death is simply the forest's way of recalling what once mattered.
